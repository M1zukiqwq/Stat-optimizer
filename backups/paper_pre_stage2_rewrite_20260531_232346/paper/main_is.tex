\title{OASIS: Feedback-Driven Marginal Statistics Correction for Cost-Based Query Optimizers}

\begin{abstract}
Cost-based optimizers depend on column-level statistics that are refreshed by
periodic \texttt{ANALYZE} passes. Between refreshes, continuous inserts,
deletes, and updates create a staleness gap: the optimizer keeps planning with
obsolete histograms even though query execution exposes predicate-level
feedback. This paper presents OASIS, a two-stage statistics-layer correction
pipeline: a learned drift-repair model first predicts corrected single-column
marginals from stale histograms and recent query feedback, and a
feedback-consistency projection then calibrates the repaired marginal before it
is exposed to downstream estimators or a DBMS planner. OASIS maps engine-native
statistics to a canonical quantile/CDF view, requires no base-table rescans, and
leaves the optimizer's search procedure and cost model unchanged.

The evaluation is optimizer-facing rather than runtime-facing, and it shows that
both stages are necessary. The learned repair stage alone reduces single-column
selectivity Q-error by 51.3\%, 57.9\%, and 60.6\% at drift intensities $q=10$,
$20$, and $30$, outperforming local-feedback methods such as ISOMER in the
moderate-to-large drift regime; such methods are constrained by observed
feedback and do not learn a reusable global drift-repair prior. The projection
stage is what makes the repaired marginal safe to compose: the stage-one
marginal alone gives only limited and unstable multi-column gains and is
\emph{actively harmful} in the bilinear FactorJoin join kernel, whereas the full
two-stage OASIS recovers stable gains. Embedded as a drop-in marginal source
across a six-estimator composition family (independence, IPF/Sinkhorn, and four
copulas), OASIS improves every estimator in that family by 22\%--38\%. In the
FactorJoin join kernel, OASIS-noProj is harmful, while the full OASIS recovers
stable join-cardinality gains. Across 12 PostgreSQL planner-only configurations,
OASIS reduces aggregate row-estimation Q-error from 27.748 to 2.360 and matches
the fresh-statistics plan shape on 96.1\% of queries, while the projection
reduces new plan deviations from 13.8\% to 1.1\%.

We then show that the Stage-2 calibration is \emph{regime-dependent}: exact
(hard) projection is best for join and planner safety, whereas a
consistency-gated \emph{soft} projection---which down-weights feedback
contradicted by the most recent observations---generalizes better for future
single-column and optimizer-facing accuracy. A residual-gated router that adds
soft projection to its candidate pool selects per case from a deployment-visible
signal: in a held-out future-predicate evaluation it lowers single-column
Q-error from 1.442 (hard projection) to 1.395 and optimizer-proxy selectivity
Q-error from 1.446 to 1.396, while matching hard projection on the FactorJoin
kernel and remaining identical to the deployed Hybrid on the PostgreSQL
plan-shape batch, because the gate never routes to soft where exactness matters. A simpler residual-gated Hybrid already
supports safe deployment under sparse or noisy feedback, cutting aggregate
DML-trace Q-error from 1.295 to 1.072. No query runtime is measured.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Cost-based optimizers (CBOs) choose query plans from estimates of predicate
selectivity, intermediate cardinality, and operator cost. In many analytical
database systems, these estimates rely on column-level statistics such as
histograms, most-common-value lists, and density summaries. The statistics are
periodically refreshed through \texttt{ANALYZE}-style procedures that read a
full or sampled view of the table. This maintenance strategy is simple and
robust, but it leaves a persistent staleness gap: tables continue to receive
inserts, deletes, and updates while the optimizer keeps planning with the last
statistics snapshot. As the gap widens, stale histograms induce systematic
selectivity errors that can propagate through the optimizer's cost model.

Existing remedies operate at different layers of the optimization stack.
Frequent \texttt{ANALYZE} reduces staleness but increases scan overhead.
Learned cardinality estimators predict point cardinalities for particular
queries and often require raw data, query workloads, or table-specific
training. Plan-level feedback systems adjust plan choices or hints after
observing execution behavior, but they do not repair the underlying statistics
objects consumed by the optimizer. This paper focuses on a complementary layer:
\emph{statistics-level correction}. Rather than replacing the optimizer or
learning complete query cardinalities, we update the column statistics supplied
to the existing CBO.

The key observation is that a DBMS already obtains useful feedback while
executing ordinary queries. For a filter predicate, the optimizer's estimated
selectivity and the executor's observed selectivity expose a local discrepancy
between the stale histogram and the current data distribution. OASIS turns a
recent window of such observations into a corrected single-column histogram
before the statistics reach the optimizer. The design is intentionally
non-invasive: OASIS requires a query-completion feedback listener and a
statistics-assembly hook, but no changes to the optimizer's search algorithm or
cost model.

The central challenge is that feedback is sparse, local, and heterogeneous.
Naively forcing a histogram to match a few recent predicates can overfit the
feedback window; ignoring the feedback leaves the prior stale. OASIS addresses
this problem by learning a \emph{drift-correction function}. It encodes the
stale quantile histogram together with recent predicate observations and uses a
compact pooled MLP to predict corrected quantile boundaries. The same
38K-parameter checkpoint is trained once on simulation-derived drift scenarios
and then reused across columns and schemas in our experiments.

Because OASIS operates at the marginal-statistics layer, its role in
multi-column settings is conditional rather than automatic. It supplies
corrected one-dimensional inputs; it does not infer dependence structure or
repair join correlations by itself. This is why OASIS has two stages. The
learned drift-repair stage extrapolates the post-drift marginal from the stale
prior and recent feedback, which is what improves estimates on future, unseen
predicates beyond the observed feedback window. Local-feedback methods are
constrained by observed predicates and lack a learned global drift prior. But a
globally plausible learned marginal need not be consistent with the predicates
that were just observed, and a downstream estimator can amplify that local
inconsistency into a much larger joint error. The second stage therefore
projects the learned marginal onto the current feedback constraints before it is
consumed by a downstream estimator or DBMS planner. Throughout, we write
OASIS-noProj for the stage-one-only output
and OASIS for the full two-stage system, which is the deployed form. The central
empirical finding is that both
stages are necessary---neither the learned stage nor a local-feedback projection
alone is sufficient across single-column accuracy and downstream composition.

A second finding concerns the Stage-2 calibration itself. Exactly matching the
observed feedback (hard projection) is the right choice before multi-column
composition and for the DBMS planner, where any residual one-dimensional error is
amplified by joins. But on future single-column predicates a softer calibration
generalizes better: it regularizes the learned marginal instead of overfitting
the exact feedback intervals. We make this soft calibration safe with a
\emph{consistency gate}---it down-weights feedback contradicted by the most
recent observations rather than cutting feedback by age---and we show that no
single Stage-2 operator dominates across both regimes. The deployed Stage 2 is
therefore a residual-gated \emph{calibrated router} that selects, per case and
from a deployment-visible feedback-residual signal, among the stale prior,
ISOMER, OASIS-noProj, hard projection, and the consistency-gated soft projection.
The router improves single-column and optimizer-facing accuracy over hard
projection while reducing exactly to hard projection on the join kernel and the
PostgreSQL planner, where the gate does not select soft. A simpler residual-gated
Hybrid over stale, ISOMER, OASIS-noProj, and OASIS remains available for sparse or
noisy feedback.

The evaluation follows this scope. We evaluate the mechanism where it acts:
future selectivity estimates, structural histogram error, downstream marginal
composition---including a six-estimator composition family and the FactorJoin
join kernel that consume OASIS as a drop-in marginal source---and real
PostgreSQL planner estimates and plan shapes. The
PostgreSQL study is planner-only: true rows are obtained with
\texttt{COUNT(*)}, and optimizer-facing evidence comes from
\texttt{EXPLAIN (FORMAT JSON)} under injected statistics states, without
measuring query runtime. To reduce dependence on a single drift simulator, we
also evaluate out-of-distribution drift families and benchmark-inspired DML
traces with explicit insert, update, and delete events.

The contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item We formulate feedback-driven single-column statistics correction as a
        DBMS-facing problem---given a stale histogram and a causal window of
        predicate feedback, produce corrected statistics that improve future
        selectivity estimates without rescanning the table---and instantiate a
        PostgreSQL-style adapter that maps MCV+histogram statistics to a
        canonical quantile/CDF view and back, isolating engine-specific logic
        from the correction model.
  \item We design OASIS, a two-stage statistics-layer pipeline: a lightweight
        learned model predicts corrected quantile boundaries from prior quantiles
        and heterogeneous feedback, and a feedback-consistency projection
        calibrates the learned marginal before it is exposed downstream.
  \item We show that both stages are necessary and that the projection is part of
        the method, not optional post-processing. The learned stage extrapolates
        drift beyond the observed feedback window, while local-feedback methods
        are constrained by observed predicates and do not learn a reusable global
        drift-repair function. Its raw output is not feedback-consistent and is
        amplified by downstream composition; in the bilinear FactorJoin join
        kernel the stage-one marginal is actively worse than stale, a failure
        the projection mitigates.
  \item We show that the Stage-2 calibration is regime-dependent and design a
        consistency-gated soft projection plus a residual-gated calibrated router
        that resolves it. The soft projection down-weights feedback contradicted
        by the most recent observations; the router selects per case from a
        deployment-visible signal, improving single-column Q-error (1.442 to
        1.395) and optimizer-proxy selectivity Q-error (1.446 to 1.396) over hard
        projection while reducing to hard projection on FactorJoin and the
        PostgreSQL plan-shape batch. We also report a negative result: a banded
        (uncertainty-relaxed) projection does not help, isolating the gain to the
        router rather than to relaxed matching.
  \item We give an optimizer-facing evaluation that measures the mechanism where
        it acts, without query runtime: single-column drift, structural and
        initial-distribution generalization, a six-estimator composition family
        (independence, IPF/Sinkhorn, and Gaussian/Clayton/Gumbel/Frank copulas)
        and the FactorJoin join kernel that consume OASIS as a drop-in marginal
        source, a 12-configuration PostgreSQL planner-only statistics-injection
        study, and deployment-safety checks under sparse, noisy, and
        trace-grounded feedback.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Column Statistics and the Staleness Gap}

We model a column histogram as an equi-depth quantile summary with $B$ buckets.
The internal boundaries $v_1,\ldots,v_{B-1}$ correspond to fixed quantile
levels $p_1,\ldots,p_{B-1}$. A range predicate such as $x < c$ is estimated by
evaluating the piecewise-linear CDF implied by those boundaries. Although real
DBMS statistics may combine histograms with auxiliary summaries such as
most-common-value lists, density vectors, or null fractions, OASIS operates on
a canonical one-dimensional CDF view.

Let $H_0$ denote the stale histogram captured at the last statistics refresh
and $H^*$ denote the current post-drift distribution. For a predicate $\sigma$,
the optimizer derives an estimate $\hat{s}_{H_0}(\sigma)$ while the executor
can later reveal an actual selectivity $s^*(\sigma)$. We measure selectivity
error using Q-error:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{QErr}(\hat{s},s^*) =
  \max\left(\frac{\hat{s}}{s^*}, \frac{s^*}{\hat{s}}\right).
  \label{eq:qerr}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Statistics Correction}

Given a stale histogram $H_0$ and an observation window
$\mathcal{O}=\{o_1,\ldots,o_K\}$, where each observation records a predicate
type, predicate boundary, estimated selectivity, and actual selectivity, the
goal is to produce corrected statistics $\hat{H}$ such that future predicates
are estimated more accurately:
\[
  \mathbb{E}_{\sigma}\left[
    \mathrm{QErr}\left(\mathrm{CDF}_{\hat{H}}(\sigma),s^*_{\sigma}\right)
  \right]
  <
  \mathbb{E}_{\sigma}\left[
    \mathrm{QErr}\left(\mathrm{CDF}_{H_0}(\sigma),s^*_{\sigma}\right)
  \right].
\]
The expectation is over future predicates from the workload distribution.

The solution must satisfy three deployment constraints. First, it must avoid
table rescans, deriving $\hat{H}$ only from $H_0$ and recent feedback. Second,
it must be cheap enough to run near query-planning time. Third, it must degrade
gracefully when feedback is sparse or uninformative, preserving the stale prior
rather than applying large unsupported corrections.

\subsection{Scope}

OASIS targets single-column histogram staleness. It does not repair
multi-column correlation errors, join-key dependence, physical design
problems, or cost-model calibration. These terms remain in the optimizer's
error budget even if the single-column histogram is accurate. The role of
OASIS is to improve the marginal statistics that many downstream estimators and
optimizers already consume.

\section{OASIS Design}
\label{sec:design}

\subsection{System Overview}

OASIS sits between the statistics store and the optimizer. When the optimizer
requests a histogram, OASIS reconstructs a canonical quantile representation,
retrieves a recent observation window for the column, predicts corrected
quantile boundaries (Stage 1), applies a feedback-consistency projection
(Stage 2), and returns an engine-native statistics object to the CBO. Query execution remains
unchanged except for lightweight per-conjunct feedback collection.

\subsection{Feature Representation}

The correction model consumes a fixed tensor containing four blocks. The prior
block stores the normalized quantile boundaries of $H_0$. The meta block stores
the null fraction, observation count ratio, and bucket count. The observation
block stores up to $K$ recent predicate observations; each observation contains
a predicate-type encoding and numeric features for predicate bounds, estimated
selectivity, actual selectivity, and recency. The mask block marks valid
observation slots. In our implementation $B=10$ and $K=16$, producing a compact
input representation.

\subsection{Correction Model}

OASIS uses a pooled MLP with a prior encoder, an observation encoder, an
attention-like pooling layer over the observation window, and a residual output
head that predicts corrections to the internal quantile boundaries. Residual
prediction encourages conservative behavior when the feedback window is
uninformative. The predicted boundaries are clamped to the normalized domain
and projected into monotone order to ensure a valid CDF.

The model is trained offline on simulation-derived compound drift. Each
training sample contains a stale prior, a causal feedback window, and
post-drift quantile targets. Once trained, the same checkpoint is reused across
columns in the evaluation; no per-table or per-distribution retuning is used.

\subsection{Feedback Collection and Integration}
\label{sec:integration}

OASIS requires two integration points. A query-completion listener records
predicate-level feedback from executor row-count instrumentation. A
statistics-assembly hook intercepts the histogram before it reaches the
optimizer and substitutes the corrected version. Because the correction is
inserted at the statistics layer, the optimizer's search procedure and cost
model remain unchanged.

\subsection{Statistics-Format Conversion}
\label{sec:mcv-adapter}

Many DBMSs do not expose a standalone editable equi-depth histogram.
PostgreSQL, for example, couples histogram boundaries with MCV lists and other
per-column summaries. We instantiate the interface for PostgreSQL-style
statistics and design it to isolate engine-specific adapters. The adapter has
three phases: reconstruct a canonical distribution view from the native object,
apply correction on the canonical view, and decompose the corrected view back
into native statistics fields. This paper validates the PostgreSQL-style
MCV+histogram instantiation; other engines require their own adapter logic.

\subsection{Stage 2: Feedback-Consistency Projection}
\label{sec:oasis-proj}

The learned stage (Stage 1) predicts a global marginal correction from learned
drift patterns; we call this stage-one-only output OASIS-noProj. For planner use,
however, the corrected CDF must also be reliable near the predicates that have
just been observed. This local requirement is especially important before
multi-column composition, where a small one-dimensional CDF mismatch can become a
larger joint-selectivity error. The second stage therefore applies a lightweight
projection after the Stage-1 prediction. The projection uses the OASIS-noProj
histogram as the starting point and adjusts it to satisfy the current feedback
constraints under an ISOMER/IPF-style consistency principle. This step remains a
single-column calibration layer; it does not estimate multi-column dependence.

\paragraph{Hard projection.}
The default Stage-2 operator builds the interval partition induced by the active
feedback predicates, initializes cell masses from the OASIS-noProj histogram, and
performs cyclic I-projections so that every active predicate's interval mass
matches its observed selectivity exactly. When sequential drift makes the active
set inconsistent, the oldest constraints are dropped first, an implicit
stale-feedback filter. We denote the resulting two-stage output OASIS; it is the
safe form for downstream composition and the DBMS planner.

\paragraph{Consistency-gated soft projection.}
Exact matching is not always optimal. On \emph{future} single-column predicates,
matching a handful of recent feedback intervals exactly can overfit them; a
softer calibration that regularizes toward the learned marginal generalizes
better. We therefore add a soft Stage-2 operator that minimizes
$\mathrm{KL}(p\,\|\,p_{\text{noProj}}) + \lambda \sum_j w_j (A_j p - y_j)^2$ over
the same partition, where $A_j$ is a feedback interval mask and $y_j$ its
observed selectivity. The crucial component is the weight $w_j$: rather than
discarding old feedback by age, we discard it by \emph{inconsistency with the
current data state}. We fit a reference distribution to the most recent
observations, score each constraint by its residual against that reference, and
set $w_j = \exp(-(\text{conflict}_j/\tau)^2)$, so feedback that the recent
observations contradict is suppressed while old-but-still-consistent feedback is
retained. This keeps the single-column generalization benefit of soft
calibration without softly fitting stale, contradicted constraints.

\paragraph{A negative result: banded projection.}
A natural alternative is to relax the equality constraints into confidence bands
$y_j\pm\delta_j$ and I-project the learned prior onto that polytope, so that
$\delta_j=0$ recovers hard projection. This does not help: widening the band
monotonically worsens single-column accuracy
(Section~\ref{sec:stage2-calibration}). The soft operator's advantage comes from
a better-conditioned cell-level optimization---it attains \emph{lower} feedback
residual than hard projection on single-column data---not from relaxed matching,
so relaxation is the wrong axis.

\paragraph{The deployed Stage 2: a calibrated router.}
No single operator dominates: hard projection is best on the join kernel and the
planner, soft projection is best on future single-column and optimizer-facing
predicates. We resolve this with a residual-gated \emph{calibrated router} that
selects, per case, the candidate with the lowest feedback residual on the current
observation window---a deployment-visible signal with no fresh labels---among the
stale prior, ISOMER, OASIS-noProj, hard projection, and consistency-gated soft
projection. Because the gate is a residual minimizer over a pool that contains
hard projection, the router can only improve on the residual signal, and it
reduces to hard projection/ISOMER on workloads (joins, the PostgreSQL planner)
where soft projection has higher residual. The output of this router is the
deployed OASIS; the simpler Hybrid over stale, ISOMER, OASIS-noProj, and OASIS
remains available where the soft candidate is unnecessary.

\section{Experimental Methodology}
\label{sec:experiments}

The evaluation asks five questions. First, does the statistics-format
conversion preserve probability mass and selectivity estimates? Second, how
much does OASIS improve single-column histogram quality under drift? Third,
when do corrected marginals compose safely with downstream multi-column
estimators, and when is feedback consistency required? Fourth, do corrected
statistics affect a real PostgreSQL optimizer pipeline in the intended
direction, without measuring runtime? Fifth, what safety checks are needed
when feedback is sparse, noisy, or trace-like rather than drawn from the
training generator?

Unless otherwise stated, OASIS is trained once on Gaussian compound drift with
$q\in\{1,3,5,10,15,20\}$ and evaluated on held-out drift intensities
$q\in\{1,3,5,10,15,20,25,30\}$. All feedback-driven methods consume the same
observation budget $K=16$ and emit corrected quantiles on the same $B=10$ grid.
We compare against the stale prior, STHoles~\cite{bruno2001stholes},
ISOMER~\cite{markl2007consistent}, QuickSel-H~\cite{park2020quicksel}, and
simple feedback heuristics. Metrics include Q-error, selectivity MAE, quantile
MAE, feedback residual, PostgreSQL estimated-row Q-error, and plan-shape match
to the fresh-statistics planner. For deployment-sensitivity diagnostics that
require many perturbed runs, we also use a transparent optimizer-decision proxy:
future predicates are generated from held-out distributions, each statistics
state selects scan and join choices under a fixed cost proxy, and the selected
choice is scored by its true proxy cost. This proxy is used only to analyze
feedback-budget and feedback-noise behavior; it is not a runtime benchmark.

\section{Single-Column Statistics Correction}
\label{sec:single-column-results}

\subsection{Format-Conversion Validation}

The PostgreSQL-style MCV+histogram conversion preserves total probability mass
exactly in the tested settings. Round-trip residual-quantile MAE is at machine
precision, with mean $2.4\times10^{-17}$ and maximum $9.2\times10^{-17}$.
Selectivity estimates remain consistent before and after conversion, with
global MAE $1.1\times10^{-4}$ and worst-case error below 0.39\%. With
PostgreSQL-like defaults, the full round trip completes in 0.066--0.073 ms.
This validates the PostgreSQL-style conversion as a low-overhead bridge between
native statistics and the canonical OASIS view.

The single-column results so far isolate Stage 1. Table~\ref{tab:single_column_projection}

\subsection{Stage-2 Calibration Variants and the Calibrated Router}
\label{sec:stage2-calibration}

The previous subsection used hard projection. We now study the Stage-2
calibration directly. This experiment reuses the cached drift data and the same
OASIS checkpoint, and evaluates each Stage-2 variant on held-out future
predicates per case (geometric-mean selectivity Q-error), together with the
feedback residual on the observation window, quantile MAE against fresh
statistics, and an optimizer-proxy join regret. It is a controlled comparison of
calibration operators, not a new training protocol.

\input{../experiments/results/stage2_calibration_20260531/table_stage2_calibration_main.tex}

Table~\ref{tab:stage2_calibration} shows the regime structure. Full-window soft
projection has the lowest single-column Q-error (1.376 versus hard 1.442) and the
lowest feedback residual, confirming that softening helps single-column
generalization rather than hurting it. But full-window soft is unsafe under
sequential drift: it softly fits stale, contradicted feedback, which (in separate
runs) inflates out-of-distribution batch-load Q-error to 1.482 versus hard 1.272.
A fixed recent window ($k{=}8$) removes that failure but discards old-but-still
consistent feedback, giving back accuracy (1.408). The consistency-gated soft
variant keeps old feedback that the recent observations do not contradict and
reaches 1.401 at a lower feedback residual than the fixed window. The calibrated
router, selecting per case by feedback residual over
$\{$stale, ISOMER, OASIS-noProj, hard, conflict-aware soft$\}$, is best overall at
1.395 with the lowest feedback residual (0.0358), lowest join regret (1.0302), and
lowest new-risk loss (1.37\% versus hard 1.81\%); it routes to soft on 44\% of
cases and to hard/ISOMER/OASIS-noProj otherwise. As a negative control, a banded
projection that relaxes the equality constraints into confidence bands
$y_j\pm\kappa\sqrt{y_j(1-y_j)}$ monotonically worsens single-column Q-error as
$\kappa$ grows (1.437, 1.450, 1.470, 1.509 for $\kappa\in\{0.04,0.10,0.20,0.35\}$),
so the gain comes from per-case routing, not from relaxed matching.

The same ordering holds on the optimizer-decision proxy of
Section~\ref{sec:experiments}: selectivity Q-error / join regret are
$1.446/1.0348$ for hard projection, $1.404/1.0312$ for conflict-aware soft,
$1.430/1.0326$ for the stale/ISOMER/noProj/hard Hybrid, and $1.396/1.0297$ for
the calibrated router---the best non-fresh result. Crucially, the router does not
trade away safety: where soft projection has higher feedback residual it is never
selected. On the FactorJoin kernel the residual-gated router stays at hard level
(join Q-error 1.0174 versus hard 1.024) while soft projection alone is worse
(1.0367); and on the PostgreSQL plan-shape batch
(Section~\ref{sec:postgres-planner}) the router is identical to the Hybrid. The
calibrated router is therefore $\ge$ hard projection on single-column and
optimizer-facing accuracy and equal to the deployed Hybrid on join and planner
safety.

\subsection{Does the Learned Stage Help After Projection?}
\label{sec:projection-init}

A reasonable concern about the two-stage design is whether the learned stage
contributes anything once the projection is applied: in several aggregate
downstream tables the full OASIS and ISOMER are close. We isolate the question
with a projection-initialization ablation. We hold the projection operator and
the feedback constraints fixed and vary only the marginal that initializes the
projection: initializing from the stale histogram reproduces ISOMER,
initializing from the learned stage gives the full OASIS, and initializing from
the post-drift marginal is a reference upper bound. All variants are scored on
held-out future predicates, not on the feedback window they were fit to.

\begingroup
\oasisUseMaxWidthResize{.96\textwidth}
\input{../experiments/results/projection_locality_20260531/table_projection_initialization.tex}
\endgroup

Table~\ref{tab:projection_initialization} shows that the learned stage is not
decorative. Holding the projection fixed, initializing from the learned marginal
lowers future-predicate Q-error from 1.536 (ISOMER) to 1.446 (full OASIS), a
5.9\% reduction, and the advantage grows monotonically with drift: $+6.8\%$,
$+10.9\%$, and $+13.0\%$ at $q=10$, $20$, and $30$, while the two methods are
within noise at $q=1$. Per-predicate, the full OASIS beats ISOMER on 55.9\% of
held-out predicates, up to 60.9\% at $q=20$. A threshold-only predicate
population gives the same pattern more mildly ($+4.3\%$ overall, $+8.8\%$ at
$q=30$).

Two further readings support the claim that both stages are necessary. First,
the columns form a clean initialization-quality ladder---stale 1.536, learned
1.446, fresh 1.157---so a better starting marginal yields a better projected
result, and the learned initialization closes about a quarter of the
ISOMER-to-fresh headroom. Second, neither stage alone reaches the two-stage
result: the raw learned marginal (OASIS-noProj, 1.645) is worse than ISOMER on
this metric, and ISOMER (projection from a stale start) is worse than the full
OASIS. This also explains why aggregate downstream tables show ISOMER and the
full OASIS closer: the learned stage's measurable advantage is on the marginal's
future-predicate accuracy, and how much of it survives depends on how strongly a
given downstream estimator's error is dominated by the marginal. A finer
locality decomposition is given in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-locality}.

\subsection{Marginal-to-Joint Composition}
\label{sec:marginal_joint}

This experiment studies composition rather than replacing multi-column
cardinality-estimation methods. OASIS repairs one-dimensional marginals, so the
question is how those repaired marginals behave when a downstream estimator
combines them under a fixed dependence assumption. We generate correlated
two-column tables with drift, correct each single-column marginal
independently, and feed the marginals into two joint estimators: independence,
the maximum-entropy estimate under only one-dimensional marginal constraints,
and a Gaussian copula with fixed correlation structure. Fresh uses post-drift
marginal inputs under the same downstream estimator; it is not an oracle joint
distribution.

\subsection{Composition-Family Generalization}
\label{sec:composition-family}

The two-column study above uses two downstream estimators. A natural concern is
whether the deployment pattern---OASIS-noProj is an unstable composition input,
and the feedback-consistency projection is required---is specific to those two
estimators or general across composition methods that consume single-column
marginals. We therefore embed OASIS as a drop-in marginal source in a family of
six independently designed composition estimators and vary only the marginal
input. The family covers the independence/maximum-entropy estimator,
iterative proportional fitting (IPF/Sinkhorn) on a two-dimensional grid, and
four copulas with distinct dependence structure: Gaussian, Clayton, Gumbel, and
Frank. To keep the comparison a clean marginal ablation and to respect the scope
boundary that OASIS does not infer dependence, the dependence structure is held
fixed and identical for every marginal input: each copula uses one association
parameter derived from the data-generating correlation, and IPF seeds its
cell-level association from a stale two-dimensional sketch and only reconciles it
with each method's corrected one-dimensional marginals. The Archimedean copulas
are intentionally mis-specified models for the Gaussian-correlated data, which
tests whether the marginal-repair benefit survives an imperfect dependence model.

\begingroup
\oasisUseMaxWidthResize{.96\textwidth}
\input{../experiments/results/composition_family_20260531/table_composition_family.tex}
\endgroup

Table~\ref{tab:composition_family} confirms both halves of the deployment story
across the whole family. OASIS-noProj again gives only weak and inconsistent
composition gains (between $+4.2\%$ and $+14.4\%$ over stale), whereas the full
OASIS improves every estimator by $22.2\%$--$38.1\%$ and Hybrid by
$22.7\%$--$38.4\%$, in each case approaching the fresh-marginal input. The gain
therefore does not depend on any single dependence model, and it persists even
under the mis-specified Archimedean copulas. This generalizes the calibration
finding from two estimators to a method family: the learned stage is a useful
input to existing composition estimators, but the feedback-consistency
projection is what makes it a stable one.

\subsection{FactorJoin Integration}
\label{sec:factorjoin}

The previous composition studies remain single-table. To test whether corrected
marginals propagate into a learned \emph{join} cardinality estimator, we embed
OASIS in the bin-based join kernel of FactorJoin~\cite{wu2023factorjoin}. For an
equi-join $A.k = B.k$, FactorJoin partitions the join-key domain into bins and
estimates the join size as
$\sum_{\text{bin}} \mathrm{cnt}_A[\text{bin}]\,\mathrm{cnt}_B[\text{bin}]/\mathrm{dv}[\text{bin}]$,
where each table's per-bin key frequency $\mathrm{cnt}_T[\text{bin}]$ is read
from a single-column histogram of its join-key distribution. We reimplement this
kernel and make the per-table key histogram the OASIS-correctable object: when
the key distribution drifts, a stale histogram yields wrong per-bin frequencies
and a wrong join estimate. The two tables' keys are generated independently, so
the only quantity that varies across methods is the quality of each single-column
key histogram; the binning, the distinct-values-per-bin assumption, and the row
counts are identical for all methods. The metric is join-cardinality Q-error
against the exact join size on the drifted data; no query runtime is measured.

\renewcommand{\small}{\scriptsize}
\input{../experiments/results/factorjoin_oasis_20260531/table_factorjoin.tex}
\endgroup

\begin{figure}[t]

  \caption{FactorJoin join-cardinality Q-error by drift intensity for the
  deployed/calibrated marginal sources. Values are the residual gap to the
  fresh-marginal floor, normalized by the stale-to-fresh gap; $0\%$ is fresh and

Table~\ref{tab:factorjoin} shows the strongest form of the calibration finding,
and Figure~\ref{fig:factorjoin} visualizes the deployable calibrated methods on
a stale-to-fresh-normalized scale.
In Table~\ref{tab:factorjoin}, OASIS-noProj does not merely underperform---it is actively harmful, raising
aggregate join Q-error above the stale baseline ($-13.5\%$, and as much as
$-22.7\%$ at moderate drift). The mechanism is structural: the FactorJoin
estimate is a bilinear functional of the two per-bin mass vectors, so it is
sensitive to mass concentration rather than to coarse range selectivity. The
learned stage, trained to minimize range-selectivity error, can return a
directionally correct but over-concentrated histogram---occasionally with
collapsed boundaries after monotone clamping---whose fine-grained mass profile
inflates $\sum_{\text{bin}} p_A p_B$ and therefore the join estimate. The
feedback-consistency projection mitigates this failure: the full OASIS improves
over stale at every tested drift level ($12.3\%$--$23.6\%$, with $15.6\%$
aggregate gain), Hybrid improves by $13.4\%$--$23.8\%$, and both closely track
the fresh-marginal input. The effect is stable as the join-bin count varies from
10 to 100. The broader lesson
sharpens the deployment boundary: the more nonlinear the downstream composition,
the more essential feedback-consistency calibration becomes, and a join estimator
should consume the full OASIS rather than the raw learned marginal.

\subsection{PostgreSQL Planner-Only Statistics Injection}
\label{sec:postgres-planner}

The previous experiments evaluate selectivity and downstream estimator

optimizer pipeline. The PostgreSQL experiment creates a synthetic table with
fresh current data, builds alternative single-column statistics for
\texttt{fact.x}, and injects them into \texttt{pg\_statistic} by copying typed
statistics rows from analyzed source tables. For each query, true cardinality
is obtained with \texttt{COUNT(*)}; PostgreSQL estimated rows and plan shape are
obtained with \texttt{EXPLAIN (FORMAT JSON)}. The experiment does not measure
query runtime.

\section{Overhead and Deployment Considerations}
\label{sec:overhead}

Per-correction OASIS inference averages 1.06 ms in our implementation,
including tensorization and one forward pass. The PostgreSQL-style
statistics-format conversion adds 0.066--0.073 ms, for a combined correction
cost of approximately 1.13 ms. Feedback collection adds bounded bookkeeping
per filter conjunct and emits one observation tuple per conjunct at query
completion. These measurements support the intended use case: maintaining
optimizer-facing statistics between scheduled statistics refreshes, not
replacing full refreshes after major schema changes or bulk data shifts.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Classical histogram construction builds statistics from data scans
\cite{ioannidis1993histogram,poosala1997selectivity}, while streaming
summaries such as KLL sketches provide compact approximate quantiles
\cite{ivkin2019kll}. Adaptive selectivity estimation has long used query
feedback to refine estimation functions~\cite{chen1994adaptive}.
Feedback-driven histograms, including self-tuning histograms, STHoles, and
ISOMER, refine statistics from observed query results
\cite{aboulnaga1999self,bruno2001stholes,markl2007consistent}. OASIS differs
by learning a reusable drift-correction function that emits DBMS-consumable
statistics rather than applying only local per-table refinement rules or
returning point selectivity estimates.

Learned cardinality estimators such as NeuroCard, Naru, DeepDB, FACE, and MSCN
predict query cardinalities using learned density or representation models
\cite{yang2020neurocard,yang2019deep,hilprecht2019deepdb,yang2020face,kipf2019learned}.
These systems are complementary to OASIS: they often output point estimates for
specific query classes, while OASIS outputs corrected marginal statistics that
can be consumed by an existing CBO. Copula-based methods and factorized join
estimators such as FactorJoin~\cite{wu2023factorjoin} similarly combine
one-dimensional marginals with a dependence or join-key model; our
composition-family and FactorJoin experiments embed OASIS as the marginal source
in exactly these estimators and show that marginal quality and feedback
consistency are prerequisites for such downstream use.

Plan-level optimization systems such as LEO, Neo, and Bao adjust plan choices
or optimizer behavior based on execution history
\cite{stillger2001leo,marcus2019neo,marcus2022bao}. OASIS operates below that
layer by correcting the statistics object itself. A system could combine both
approaches: statistics correction supplies better base-table inputs, while
plan-level learning handles residual cost-model or join-order effects.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

The evaluation is limited to tested synthetic drift families, benchmark-inspired
DML traces, and controlled PostgreSQL planner-only scenarios. The DML traces
make insert/update/delete event streams explicit, but they are not production
logs or DBMS telemetry; validating the same correction policy on production
statistics histories and workload traces remains future work. The PostgreSQL
batch covers two drift directions, two table sizes, and three random seeds, so
it validates the statistics-to-planner path more robustly than a single case,
but it does not establish wall-clock runtime improvement. Runtime depends on
executor behavior, caching, physical design, system load, and cost-model
calibration. OASIS also does not infer multi-column dependence from
single-column feedback. When downstream estimates depend on correlations, an
external dependence model or multivariate statistics source is still required.
Finally, feedback itself can be noisy because of predicate attribution,
sampling, or concurrent updates. Our noise diagnostic shows graceful
degradation up to 10\% multiplicative feedback noise, but deployments should
pair correction with residual-based gating, abstention, and rollback policies.
The calibrated router also gates on feedback residual, which is a proxy for
estimate quality rather than for plan-shape risk: on the PostgreSQL subset it
prefers ISOMER and so inherits the Hybrid's slightly higher new-deviation rate
(8.17\% versus 6.86\% for hard projection alone), even though it never routes to
the soft candidate there. A plan-shape-aware gating criterion that could route to
hard projection over ISOMER on planner-facing cases is left to future work.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

OASIS repairs stale single-column optimizer statistics from query feedback
without rescanning tables or modifying the optimizer. It is a two-stage pipeline:
a compact learned model predicts corrected quantile boundaries from a stale prior
and recent observations, and a feedback-consistency projection then calibrates
the learned marginal before it is exposed downstream; a PostgreSQL-style adapter
connects the canonical correction view to DBMS-native statistics.

The central lesson is that both stages are necessary, and they answer
complementary failure modes. The learned stage extrapolates drift to future,
unseen predicates by using a learned global drift prior, while local-feedback
methods such as ISOMER are constrained by observed feedback. This drives the
single-column gains across synthetic drift, OOD families, and distribution
transfer. But a globally plausible learned marginal need not be consistent with
the predicates just observed, and downstream estimators amplify that
inconsistency: the stage-one marginal gives only weak and unstable multi-column
gains and is actively harmful in the bilinear FactorJoin join kernel. The
projection stage mitigates this downstream failure, at the cost of a small amount
of single-column peak accuracy. A further lesson is that this Stage-2 calibration
is regime-dependent: hard projection is best for join and planner safety, while a
consistency-gated soft projection---which down-weights feedback contradicted by
the most recent observations---generalizes better on future single-column and
optimizer-facing predicates. Because no single operator dominates, the deployed
Stage 2 is a residual-gated calibrated router that selects per case from a
deployment-visible signal; it lowers single-column Q-error from 1.442 to 1.395 and
optimizer-proxy selectivity Q-error from 1.446 to 1.396 over hard projection,
recovering the projection's single-column cost, while reducing exactly to hard
projection or ISOMER on the join kernel and PostgreSQL planner, where it never
routes to soft. A banded relaxation of the equality constraints was a negative
control that localizes the gain to routing rather than to relaxed matching.
Because OASIS acts at the marginal layer, it embeds as a
drop-in marginal source for existing composition estimators, improving every
member of a six-estimator family by 22\%--38\%. In FactorJoin, OASIS-noProj is
harmful, while the full OASIS recovers stable join-cardinality gains. In the
12-configuration PostgreSQL planner-only batch, OASIS reduces aggregate
row-estimation Q-error from 27.748 to 2.360, matches the fresh-statistics plan
shape on 96.1\% of queries, and keeps new plan deviations near 1\%---where the
learned stage alone would introduce them---without measuring or claiming query
runtime improvement. A residual-gated Hybrid policy, choosing among stale,
ISOMER, OASIS-noProj, and OASIS from the current feedback residual, reduces
aggregate trace-grounded Q-error from 1.295 to 1.072. This makes OASIS a
practical component for maintaining optimizer-facing statistics between scheduled
\texttt{ANALYZE} passes.

\label{sec:appendix-locality}

To see where the learned stage's advantage comes from, we bin the held-out
future predicates of Section~\ref{sec:projection-init} by their distance to the
feedback window. Each predicate endpoint is mapped to fresh-CDF space and
assigned the directed Hausdorff distance to the nearest feedback endpoint,
giving near ($\leq 0.05$), mid ($\leq 0.15$), and far ($>0.15$) bins.

\endgroup

Table~\ref{tab:feedback_locality} reports the diagnostic on the mixed predicate
population. The full OASIS beats ISOMER in every bin, and---restricting to the
operative drift regime $q\geq5$---by $+9.2\%$, $+6.9\%$, and $+8.0\%$ in the
near, mid, and far bins. The advantage is therefore not confined to predicates
near the feedback window. The raw learned marginal (OASIS-noProj) is, by
contrast, worse than ISOMER in every bin and worst in the far bin, confirming
that raw learned marginals are not reliably deployable across locality bins; the
projection turns the learned initialization into a stable statistics object.

The far bin is predicate-type dependent. For a threshold-only population (pure
$<$/$>$ predicates, which probe only a cumulative CDF level), the far-bin
advantage shrinks to $+0.8\%$ at $q\geq5$, while near and mid remain strong
($+7.7\%$ and $+5.4\%$). Range and point predicates, which probe local density
rather than a cumulative level, retain the far-bin advantage. The learned
stage's value in uncovered regions therefore comes from better local density,
which helps range and point queries but adds little to purely cumulative
threshold queries.
